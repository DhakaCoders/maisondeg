Our history since 1848

Ever since Maison De Greef was founded in 1848, it has perpetuated the values of creation, curation, excellence and transmission. Six generations, each with their own unique vision, have allowed our family business to prosper, preserving the heritage and identity of our House for almost two centuries.

Maison De Greef has evolved with the times to continue to satisfy our customers' wishes. We see the coming decades as full of dreamlike creativity, modernity and an insatiable appetite for new challenges.

  • 1848-1880

    François-Prosper De Greef founded the eponymous house in 1848 at 28 Rue au Beurre*, just a stone's throw from Brussels' Grand Place. He soon laid the foundations for enduring success by becoming an accredited clockmaker for the Belgian National Railway Company and subsequently an official supplier of pocket watches to the Belgian Navy, the Postal Administration, and the Telegraph and Telephone Administration.

  • 1880-1903

    From 1880, Léon De Buck and his wife Agnès were in direct contact with the House's clientèle. The couple devoted their time to listening to and advising customers to truly satisfy the wishes of everyone who stepped foot in the boutique. As it sold jewellery that catered to contemporary tastes, pocket watches, silverware and other precious metal items, Maison De Greef became revered for its elegance and precious objects in belle-époque Brussels.

    In 1897, the couple decided to move the jewellery shop to the premises of a former pharmacy at 26 Rue au Beurre to gain space and offer a wider selection of fine jewellery to customers of the House.

  • 1903-1959

    In 1903, 30-year-old Augusta De Buck, wife of Jules Wittmann, took over the family jewellery shop in exchange for a life annuity for her mother – now widowed – and her brother Paul. In 1929 Augusta De Buck, now a widow herself, nonetheless reaped the rewards of 25 years of work, becoming sole owner of the family business. A dynamic, strong-minded woman and an excellent strategist, she purchased number 26 and, in 1935, number 24 Rue au Beurre, thereby giving the company fresh momentum.

  • 1903-1959

    In 1953, Augusta, assisted by her eldest son Jean, decided to have the façades of numbers 24 and 26 revamped by modernist architect Jacques Dupuis. His plans proved visionary, with a single flattened cement façade painted in white with jet-black window frames. The smallest grand boutique in Brussels now boasted an elegant, pure, timeless and incredibly avant-garde look.

    Inside, the spaces were arranged fluidly and organically. Mirrors drew the eye towards the space's more confidential areas, while the public portion opened into an expansive empty space adorned with a large Murano glass chandelier.

    Augusta's sons Jean and Marcel became increasingly involved in the family business and took over from her in 1959 after her death.
